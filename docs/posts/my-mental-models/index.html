When I came across the book “Poor Charlie’s Almanack”, I thought it was the best book I had ever read. It still remains one of the best ones. The most important idea I took from that book was to have a latticework of mental models and to regularly use those when thinking about problems. Since then, I have been trying to collect such models. In this post, I have tried to list down the models that I have collected- these are just the models that help me understand the world a bit better, and by no means this is everyone’s list. The models that are listed here come from a variety of disciplines such as Psychology, Economics, Computer Science, and Mathematics. Feel free to suggest or share some other mental models that I should include in this list by reaching me here. I have tried to categorize these models but at times you can find overlap across categories. Over time, I intend to add to this list.

Models from Psychology

1. Survivorship Bias

History is told by the victors. Survivorship bias focuses more on those who survived and less on those who did not. For example, if a fund managing firm is reporting only the performance of its funds that have survived, it’s going to be a flawed representation. We would also want to know which funds failed! Here’s a pretty good explanation: Eddie Woo on Survivorship Bias.

2. Confirmation Bias

Often, we don’t consciously register that we have formed an opinion or a decision, but our mind leads us to find evidence that supports what we believe. For example, if your mind supports a particular politician, you’re much more likely to search for or interpret the evidence that supports your position!

3. Availability Heuristic

Availability heuristic is the tendency that we assign higher probability to the events that we can easily recall. The easiest example given is of terrorism- we can recall terrorism instances easily, so we think they happen often. But there are worse things than terrorism that happen far too often that we don’t recall easily. So, the next time we are using the word always, we ought to be more careful. A related model is that of the recency effect, where we place more importance to the events that happened recently. If you had to give someone a raise, most likely you gave it based on their recent performance.

4. Hindsight Bias

Looking back, things are always obvious. But when they are happening, they’s not entirely so. During an event, we don’t have full information about it. But we do get it after the event has occurred. It’s very easy to say, “I should’ve known”, but how could you have?!

5. Loss Aversion

Humans have a tendency to weigh losses more heavily than profits. For example, a 100$ loss hurts more than what 100$ gain gives pleasure.

6. Halo Effect

If you like someone, you’re more likely to see the positive things in them. It’s not the other way around- you don’t like someone because they have positive qualities. You see positive qualities because you like them!

7. Reciprocity and Cooperation

Humans are wired to reciprocate! If you do something for me, I feel compelled to do something of equal value for you.

8. Familiarity Effect

The more familiar you are to something, the more you see it as credible. Imagine just by showing us an image several times, we can be fooled into believing it as true! Ever seen politicians do this?

9. Parkinson’s Law

Constraints are a source of creativity. The looser the constraints, the poorer the performance you’re going to get. We can see Parkinson’s law in action when we deal with deadlines: the bigger the deadline, the longer it takes to finish the task.

2. Inversion

Inversion simply means starting from the output that you want and going back to the input that is required. If you want to reach some output, start at the outputs and then figure out what inputs need to be given to reach the output. As a simple example, think about a goal that you have in life, and start thinking backwards from the goal. Another interpretation of the inversion model is that instead of trying to find out what works, try to find out what doesn’t work and avoid those things. Both ways are pretty good models.

Models from Economics

1. Sunk Costs

2. Opportunity Cost

This is my favorite model to use. It means that if you’re doing something, then that means you’re not doing something else. So truly, the what you are not doing needs to be added to the cost of what you are doing. If you’re spending time watching Instagram, then not only is the current time the cost, but also the time that you will spend in future to do the things you should be doing.

3. Elasticity

4. Margin of Safety

If someone told you that a bridge has a capacity of 1000kg only. Then would you drive your 980kg car over that bridge? Probably not. That’s margin of safety. It’s an elusive concept to define but very simple to understand if you don’t care about exact definitions. Whatever our estimates or predictions are, they can be wrong. And we should bet only if we can win even by being wrong!

5. Moats

Moats are way of thinking about how safe and long-lasting something is. What protections does it have, and how sustainable are these protections? You can interpret it in whichever way fits your needs. For instance, if you’re a job seeker, your moat could be your skills. What are your skills that are protected and how long can you protect them from others? If you can’t do this for long, you probably need to work on building a bigger moat.

Models from the Sciences

1. A Catalyst

A catalyst is something that speeds up the process. If you’re a student, getting a job from a connection’s referral might feel like you’re not building your career on your own. But why not think of it this way: you’re using a catalyst to launch your career into a bigger orbit?

2. Natural Selection

Only the fittest survive, and over time the traits that remain are the ones that helped survival. There are three important questions when thinking about evolution by natural selection:

- How fast is the replication?
- How long is the life?
- How accurate is the replication?

Models from Computer Science

1. Decoupling

The less dependencies there are in a system, the less likely it is to collapse. Even if it does, it can recover faster.

2. Goodhart’s Law

This may be more of a quote than a model but I think it captures an important idea: “When a metric becomes a target, it ceases to be a good metric”. Metrics are heuristics- not absolute truths to compromise everything after!
